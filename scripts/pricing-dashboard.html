Paris Pricing Dashboard

Paris boutique fitness pricing looks standardized – but underneath, the economics are wildly different.

Why This Matters

Pricing is one of the most foundational and least questioned elements of how boutique fitness studios operate.

Over the past decade, pricing in Paris has quietly converged toward a narrow range, regardless of modality, cost structure, or class capacity. While studios differ widely in format and economics, the prices they charge are often strikingly similar.

This report analyzes 228 studios (~350 locations) across Paris to understand how pricing is structured today, from drop-ins to memberships, and what that means for how these businesses actually operate.

Pricing Clusters

Paris boutique fitness pricing clusters into a few clearly defined zones.

The majority of studios fall within a narrow range of €25 to €35 per class, effectively defining what “boutique fitness” costs in Paris today.

Below this sits a smaller budget segment (€18-€21), while a new premium tier is emerging above €45, primarily driven by Reformer Pilates studios.

Despite differences in concept, location, and cost structure, most studios price within a surprisingly tight band.

Nearly half of studios fall within a €10 range (€25-€35)

However, when we look at geographic distribution of drop-in rates, we actually discover rather stark differences.

The west (in particular the 7th, 8th, 16th, and 17th arrondissements) have average drop-in rates between €34 and €39, while in the east and south drop-in rates average about €15 lower.

We also see that more expensive areas have a higher density of studios, creating true hot zones for boutique fitness.

Pricing by Modality

At a surface level, pricing appears to follow equipment intensity. Modalities like dance and martial arts, which require little to no equipment, tend to charge less, while cycling and reformer, which require significant upfront investment, charge more.

But the reality is more surprising. What differentiates these modalities is more than upfront investment: it's also space required and capacity, both of which have huge impacts on the studio's business model.

Nonetheless, across most modalities, pricing still clusters around €25-€30, suggesting that studios are anchoring to a shared market expectation rather than pricing purely based on underlying costs.

For years, many operators assumed that ~€30 represented a pricing ceiling. That assumption has had lasting implications for how studios design their business models. This assumption is starting to be questioned and broken by trends such as Reformer Pilates / Lagree and to a lesser extent Aqua Cycling.

Intro Offers

Introductory offers are widely used across the market to attract first-time customers.

These offers are typically steeply discounted, reflecting the cost of customer acquisition in an increasingly competitive landscape.

Two formats dominate:

  • A single discounted first class
  • A 3-class introductory pack

These formats reflect different conversion strategies. Lower-priced studios often rely on single-class trials, while more premium studios lean toward multi-class packs, recognizing that it may take multiple visits to convert a new client.

Average intro discount: ~40% vs drop-in price

Packages

Class packages remain a core pricing mechanism across boutique fitness studios in Paris.

The structure of these packages is remarkably consistent. The 10-class pack is the most common offering, and larger packages are typically incentivized with higher discounts.

Discounting follows predictable patterns, generally ranging from ~10% to ~35% depending on pack size.

Expiration policies are also highly standardized:

  • Small packs (5 classes): ~2-3 months
  • Mid-size packs (10 classes): ~6 months
  • Large packs: ~12 months

This consistency suggests a strong level of market convergence, not just in pricing, but in product design.

Memberships

As the industry matures, studios are gradually shifting toward subscription-based memberships. However, adoption remains uneven.

Nearly 30% of studios offer no memberships at all, continuing to rely primarily on class packs.

Among those that do offer memberships, most follow a familiar structure:

  • 4, 8, or 12 classes per month
  • Unlimited memberships

Interestingly, many studios offer memberships with no long-term commitment, even when pricing is heavily discounted.

This creates a potential tension: memberships are often discounted significantly more than class packages, yet without requiring the stability that long-term commitments typically provide.

Revenue per Class

While pricing across boutique fitness has largely converged, the underlying economics of these businesses vary dramatically.

Different modalities operate with very different class capacities, which directly impacts how much revenue each class can generate.

Studios offering high-capacity classes, such as cycling or HIIT, can generate significantly more revenue per session than lower-capacity formats like reformer or aerial.

At similar price points, this can result in 2-3x differences in revenue per class.

This has major implications for:

  • profitability
  • studio size and layout
  • scheduling flexibility
  • pricing strategy

Reformer Case Study

Reformer Pilates stands out as one of the few modalities that has successfully broken away from the industry’s historical pricing ceiling.

While most boutique fitness classes cluster around €25-€30, Reformer studios frequently charge €35-€45+ per class.

This is significant because Reformer classes typically operate with much lower capacity, making higher pricing essential to the viability of the model.

The success of Reformer suggests that pricing ceilings in boutique fitness may be more flexible than previously assumed – when paired with strong positioning and product-market fit.

Several factors have enabled this shift:

  • Premium positioning and branding
  • Efficient use of smaller studio spaces
  • A target clientele willing to pay for a specialized experience
Reformer is the clearest example that €30 is not a true ceiling
