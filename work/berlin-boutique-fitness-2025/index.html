What is this report?

Berlin is one of the most developed and dynamic boutique fitness markets in Europe — often setting trends that ripple to other global cities. While boutique fitness remains inherently trend-driven and still evolving even in Berlin, this report provides a data-driven snapshot of the market as of 2025.

Using a large and representative dataset of boutique fitness studios across Berlin, this analysis explores:

  • Market size and growth
  • Evolution of key modalities
  • Patterns of market maturity
  • Geographic distribution and clustering

Rather than exact studio counts, this report emphasizes relative market shares, growth trajectories, and spatial trends to deliver meaningful insights for operators, investors, and planners.

Multi-location studios count each location separately. Note on 2014: The .berlin top-level domain launched on 17 February 2014, and many studios registered a .berlin address on that day. This inflates 2014 openings slightly — some of those studios predate 2014 but their domain registration date falls on launch day.

Berlin's boutique fitness growth story is one of steady, moderate expansion rather than the explosive curves seen in cities like London or Paris. Studios have been opening consistently since the mid-2000s, with a noticeable acceleration in the early 2010s, a spike around 2014, and a clear COVID dip in 2020. Post-COVID recovery is visible but not dramatic — the market is growing, but at a measured pace that likely reflects both the saturation of a city with a deeply established gym culture and the fact that a meaningful share of studios have no dated digital footprint in our dataset. As with all date-based analyses, these are signals rather than a complete census.

Yoga leads Berlin's boutique fitness market with over 400 studios, followed by pilates, strength training, and boxing. The early years of boutique fitness (prior to 2012) saw a fairly even spread of openings across modalities, but yoga and pilates have since pulled clearly ahead. Yoga and pilates tracked closely together for most of the industry's life but have diverged recently, with pilates booming — largely driven by reformer — since 2019.

Multi-modality studios are everywhere. Only —% of studios offer just one modality, while the majority (—%) offer multiple. But with more and more unique and hybrid concepts, even the idea of a single modality needs to be questioned. Rather, we are starting to see variations on multi-modality: classes fusing different exercises into one, flagship locations with open gym space, and of course studios offering a primary modality but filling extra space and time with complementary classes.

A note on categorisation

Dance & Martial Arts

Berlin has a large number of dance and martial arts spaces — but including them wholesale in the boutique fitness analysis would be misleading.

Berlin's data contains — studios whose primary activity is dance or martial arts. These span a wide spectrum: on one end, contemporary dance studios and combat fitness gyms that operate on a drop-in, package, or monthly membership model that is indistinguishable from mainstream boutique fitness; on the other, traditional schools — ballet academies, dojos, ballroom studios — that tend to cater heavily to children, operate on semester or annual enrolment, and have been running for decades.

Including these in the main modality charts would inflate the numbers significantly and distort the picture of what Berliners are actually doing in boutique fitness. Studios that offer dance or martial arts alongside yoga, pilates, strength training, or barre are included in the main analysis under those other categories. The — studios whose entire offering is dance and/or martial arts are shown here separately.

Kreuzberg, Prenzlauer Berg, and Mitte lead Berlin's boutique fitness density — each shaped by different neighbourhood dynamics.

Kreuzberg, Prenzlauer Berg, and Mitte have the greatest concentration of boutique studios. Kreuzberg's density reflects its long-standing role as a creative and alternative lifestyle hub, while Prenzlauer Berg's high count tracks the district's gentrification wave and influx of wellness-oriented families. Mitte benefits from central location and high daytime population. Charlottenburg and Wilmersdorf represent more traditional, affluent catchments in the west with steady demand.
